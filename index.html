The human brain is the central organ of the nervous system and serves as the command center for the entire body. Weighing approximately 1.3 to 1.4 kilograms in adults, it contains nearly 86 billion neurons interconnected through trillions of synapses. The brain is divided into major regions including the cerebrum, cerebellum, and brainstem, each responsible for specific physiological and cognitive functions. Protected by the skull and cushioned by cerebrospinal fluid, the brain operates through complex electrochemical signaling. It regulates voluntary actions such as movement and speech, as well as involuntary processes like breathing, heart rate, and hormonal balance. Cerebrum

The cerebrum is the largest and most complex portion of the human brain, accounting for nearly 85% of total brain mass. It is divided into two hemispheres connected by the corpus callosum. Structurally, the outer layer known as the cerebral cortex consists of gray matter composed of neuron cell bodies, while underlying white matter contains myelinated axons that facilitate communication. The surface appears folded into gyri and sulci, increasing surface area for higher cognitive processing. Functionally, the cerebrum is responsible for voluntary movement, reasoning, language, emotion, perception, and decision-making. Each hemisphere controls the opposite side of the body. The left hemisphere is generally dominant for language and analytical tasks, whereas the right hemisphere contributes to spatial awareness and creativity. Clinically, cerebrum damage can lead to paralysis, speech disorders such as aphasia, memory loss, or personality changes. Strokes commonly affect cerebral arteries, disrupting blood supply and causing localized brain injury. Interestingly, despite lateralization, both hemispheres constantly communicate, allowing integrated and coordinated behavior.

Brain Lobes

The cerebrum is subdivided into four major lobes: frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital. The frontal lobe, located at the front of the brain, governs executive functions, personality, planning, and voluntary motor activity. The parietal lobe processes sensory information including touch, temperature, and spatial awareness. The temporal lobe, positioned laterally, plays a vital role in auditory processing, language comprehension, and memory formation. The occipital lobe at the back of the brain specializes in visual processing. Damage to specific lobes produces predictable clinical outcomes. Frontal lobe injury may result in impaired judgment and behavioral changes. Parietal damage can cause loss of spatial orientation. Temporal lobe dysfunction may affect memory and hearing, while occipital injury leads to visual disturbances. An interesting fact is that the frontal lobe continues developing into early adulthood, explaining changes in decision-making maturity during adolescence.

Brainstem

The brainstem consists of the midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata. It connects the brain to the spinal cord and regulates essential life-sustaining functions. The midbrain coordinates visual and auditory reflexes. The pons acts as a bridge relaying signals between cerebrum and cerebellum. The medulla oblongata controls respiration, heart rate, and blood pressure. Clinically, brainstem damage is life-threatening because it can disrupt breathing and cardiac regulation. Conditions such as brainstem stroke or tumors may result in coma or locked-in syndrome. Despite its small size compared to the cerebrum, the brainstem is crucial for survival, highlighting its fundamental role in autonomic control.
