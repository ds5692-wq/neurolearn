Cerebrum

Location & Structure: The cerebrum is the largest brain structure, occupying the uppermost part of the cranial cavity. It is divided into two hemispheres by the longitudinal fissure and covered by the cerebral cortex composed of gray matter, while the inner layer consists of white matter. The surface is folded into gyri and sulci, increasing surface area.

Functions: Responsible for voluntary motor control, sensory perception, reasoning, decision-making, language, emotion, and higher cognitive functions.

Clinical Significance: Damage may cause paralysis, aphasia, memory deficits, or personality changes. Strokes affecting cerebral arteries are common clinical concerns.

Interesting Facts: Despite being only ~2% of body weight, the cerebrum consumes ~20% of total energy.

Cerebellum

Location & Structure: Located below the cerebrum and behind the brainstem, the cerebellum has two hemispheres connected by the vermis. Its cortex contains tightly folded gray matter, and the underlying white matter forms a tree-like pattern called the arbor vitae.

Functions: Coordinates voluntary movements, maintains posture, balance, and motor learning.

Clinical Significance: Damage may cause ataxia, tremors, or difficulty in coordinating movements. Cerebellar strokes are less common but significant.

Interesting Facts: The cerebellum contains over 50% of all neurons in the brain despite being smaller than the cerebrum.

Brainstem

Location & Structure: The brainstem connects the brain with the spinal cord and consists of the midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata. It contains nuclei, fiber tracts, and cranial nerve origins.

Functions: Regulates heart rate, respiration, consciousness, and reflexes; relays motor and sensory signals between brain and body.

Clinical Significance: Brainstem lesions can cause life-threatening dysfunction, coma, or cranial nerve deficits.

Interesting Facts: The brainstem is evolutionarily the oldest part of the brain, responsible for basic life functions.

Frontal Lobe

Location & Structure: Located at the front of the cerebrum, anterior to the central sulcus. It contains the prefrontal cortex, motor cortex, and Broca‚Äôs area in the left hemisphere.

Functions: Executive functions, planning, reasoning, voluntary movement, problem-solving, and speech production.

Clinical Significance: Damage can result in impaired judgment, personality changes, difficulty in planning, or expressive aphasia.

Interesting Facts: The frontal lobe continues developing into early adulthood, explaining the gradual maturation of executive functions.

Parietal Lobe

Location & Structure: Positioned posterior to the frontal lobe, above the temporal lobe. The postcentral gyrus serves as the primary somatosensory cortex.

Functions: Processes touch, temperature, pain, spatial awareness, and integrates sensory information.

Clinical Significance: Lesions may cause sensory deficits, neglect syndrome, or impaired spatial orientation.

Interesting Facts: Plays a key role in mapping the body in space and coordinating hand-eye tasks.

Temporal Lobe

Location & Structure: Located beneath the lateral sulcus on the sides of the cerebrum. Includes auditory cortex, hippocampus, and Wernicke‚Äôs area in the left hemisphere.

Functions: Auditory perception, language comprehension, memory formation, and emotional processing.

Clinical Significance: Damage can cause receptive aphasia, memory deficits, or auditory hallucinations.

Interesting Facts: Houses the hippocampus, essential for long-term memory consolidation.

Occipital Lobe

Location & Structure: Situated at the back of the cerebrum, containing the primary visual cortex along the calcarine fissure.

Functions: Visual perception, color recognition, motion detection, and processing of visual information.

Clinical Significance: Lesions may result in blindness, visual field defects, or visual agnosia.

Interesting Facts: Despite its small size, it is crucial for interpreting the visual world.

Thalamus

Location & Structure: A paired, egg-shaped structure deep within the diencephalon, located above the brainstem. Composed of multiple nuclei transmitting sensory and motor information.

Functions: Relays sensory signals to the cortex, regulates consciousness, sleep, and alertness.

Clinical Significance: Thalamic stroke may cause sensory loss, coma, or cognitive deficits.

Interesting Facts: Often referred to as the brain‚Äôs ‚Äúrelay station.‚Äù

Hypothalamus

Location & Structure: Located below the thalamus, forming the floor of the third ventricle. Contains multiple nuclei controlling autonomic and endocrine functions.

Functions: Regulates hunger, thirst, body temperature, sleep-wake cycle, and hormone secretion via pituitary gland.

Clinical Significance: Lesions may cause hormonal imbalance, appetite disorders, or temperature dysregulation.

Interesting Facts: It is one of the smallest but most important brain structures for survival.

Pituitary Gland

Location & Structure: Small pea-sized gland at the base of the brain, connected to the hypothalamus by the pituitary stalk.

Functions: Master endocrine gland regulating growth, metabolism, reproduction, and stress response through hormone secretion.

Clinical Significance: Tumors can cause gigantism, Cushing‚Äôs disease, or hormonal imbalances.

Interesting Facts: Often called the ‚Äúmaster gland‚Äù for controlling other endocrine glands.

Corpus Callosum

Location & Structure: Thick bundle of nerve fibers connecting the left and right cerebral hemispheres. Composed of over 200 million axons.

Functions: Enables interhemispheric communication, coordination, and integration of cognitive functions.

Clinical Significance: Agenesis may cause split-brain syndrome or cognitive impairments.

Interesting Facts: Severing it in epilepsy patients can stop seizures but affects coordination between hemispheres.

Neurons (Structure & Function)

Location & Structure: Neurons are the fundamental units of the brain and nervous system. Each neuron consists of a cell body (soma), dendrites that receive signals, and an axon that transmits impulses. Synaptic terminals connect neurons for communication via neurotransmitters.

Functions: Responsible for receiving, processing, and transmitting electrical and chemical signals throughout the body. Enable cognition, sensation, movement, and autonomic processes.

Clinical Significance: Damage to neurons or synapses can lead to neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer‚Äôs, Parkinson‚Äôs, or peripheral neuropathies.

Interesting Facts: The human brain contains ~86 billion neurons, each forming thousands of synapses, creating trillions of connections.

Limbic System

Location & Structure: Located deep within the cerebrum, including the hippocampus, amygdala, cingulate gyrus, and hypothalamus. It forms a ring around the thalamus.

Functions: Responsible for emotion, motivation, memory formation, and behavior regulation.

Clinical Significance: Damage may cause emotional disturbances, memory loss, or mood disorders.

Interesting Facts: Often called the "emotional brain" because it processes fear, pleasure, and social behaviors.

Hippocampus

Location & Structure: A seahorse-shaped structure within the temporal lobe. Composed of dense gray matter and curved layers called cornu ammonis.

Functions: Crucial for forming new memories and spatial navigation.

Clinical Significance: Damage leads to anterograde amnesia and difficulties in forming new memories.

Interesting Facts: One of the first regions affected in Alzheimer‚Äôs disease.

Amygdala

Location & Structure: Almond-shaped clusters located in the medial temporal lobes. Consists of multiple nuclei connected to sensory pathways.

Functions: Processes fear, anxiety, emotional learning, and memory modulation.

Clinical Significance: Dysfunction may result in anxiety disorders, PTSD, or impaired emotional responses.

Interesting Facts: Known as the brain‚Äôs ‚Äúfear center‚Äù but also important for positive emotions.

Basal Ganglia

Location & Structure: Deep gray matter nuclei including caudate, putamen, globus pallidus, subthalamic nucleus, and substantia nigra. Located near the lateral ventricles.

Functions: Controls voluntary movement, motor learning, habit formation, and procedural memory.

Clinical Significance: Parkinson‚Äôs disease and Huntington‚Äôs disease arise from basal ganglia dysfunction.

Interesting Facts: Plays a role in reward processing and habit formation beyond movement control.

Broca's Area

Location & Structure: Located in the posterior part of the left frontal lobe, near the lateral sulcus. Composed of specialized cortical neurons.

Functions: Critical for speech production, articulation, and language processing.

Clinical Significance: Damage leads to expressive aphasia: understanding intact but difficulty speaking.

Interesting Facts: Named after Paul Broca, who discovered its role in speech in the 1860s.

Wernicke's Area

Location & Structure: Located in the posterior section of the superior temporal gyrus in the left hemisphere. Rich in cortical neurons for auditory processing.

Functions: Language comprehension, processing spoken and written communication.

Clinical Significance: Damage causes receptive aphasia: speech fluent but meaning impaired.

Interesting Facts: Named after Carl Wernicke, a German neurologist who identified its role in comprehension.
